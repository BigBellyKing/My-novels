The current game content definitely cannot be officially launched.

Some features in the game have not yet been implemented, and the development of the MOD tools is still undergoing internal debugging.

Additionally, there's the review process for the Workshop.

Before this, the main MOD content in the Workshop consisted of Overcooked and Fall Guys. The MODs for these two games, developed by players, were essentially components based on the games' existing assets.

So, there wouldn't be any major issues. But now it's different, especially with the previous release of Don't Starve. For this game, Chen Xu has granted more permissions to players regarding MODs.

In fact, if players wish, they can not only modify some text descriptions in the game but also make higher-level changes to the game's content rules.

Of course, whether these changes are good or bad, interesting or not, will depend on the individual ability of the MOD creators.

But this has also made Don't Starve's MOD content more complex.

As for Minecraft, its nature is even more prominent compared to Don't Starve.

Therefore, constant vigilance is required for the Workshop and the player community.

The sheer volume of content in this area is quite large, so a dedicated review team is also needed here.

According to Chen Xu's plan, Minecraft will be launched in early February, which is around the New Year.

At the same time, a small-scale beta test will be released after New Year's Day, specifically to gather player feedback on the game content and their experiences with various details.

For example, facing a completely free game mode like Minecraft, can players understand how to play it? Do they need any beginner tutorials to help them grasp the basic operations of the game in the early stages?

The period between the end of testing and the game's launch will be the allocated time.

Of course, even if modifications are needed, they will mostly be limited to controls and tutorials.

This is because the overall gameplay and framework of the game have already been decided and will not change.

As Minecraft's content gradually solidified,

The market also saw the arrival of many sandbox games.

Various developers were scrambling to promote their titles, hoping to capitalize on the sandbox game craze before it faded.

Amidst the overwhelming promotions, the games from major developers NetDragon and TengHua garnered the most attention.

Although not yet launched, they were highly anticipated by many players.

As for the reason?

It's very simple: as major game developers, TengHua and NetDragon allocated sufficient budgets for the entirely new genre of sandbox games.

While such a budget was merely a trial run for TengHua and NetDragon, given their extensive resources, even a trial run budget far surpassed what ordinary companies could afford.

This led to considerable anticipation from players for TengHua and NetDragon's new titles, Voyage and Wasteland.

The former takes a seafaring adventure as its entry point, while the latter features a post-apocalyptic worldview.

TengHua's Voyage primarily focuses on adventure content, marketed as the first seafaring sandbox game, while NetDragon's entry emphasizes post-apocalyptic survival.

The specific gameplay content remains unclear, as not much has been revealed beyond their respective themes.

But it must be said, they are indeed very appealing to players.

While there are many wasteland-themed games, few focus purely on survival. Seafaring themes are even rarer. Adding sandbox elements immediately made these two games stand out.

The producer of NetDragon's Wasteland was an old acquaintance of Chen Xu, the warm-hearted Mr. Meng.

Among games released concurrently, these two titles definitely captured players' attention.

After all, with good graphics, major developer backing, and compelling selling points, it was naturally hard for them not to attract players.

During this period, following Don't Starve, several second and third-tier game developers also released their new titles.

However, most of these were simply jumping on the bandwagon, or even reskinning old games and labeling them as sandbox games.

They were heavily criticized by players, with the highest rating only reaching 7 points.

Yet, precisely because of this, the industry became even more optimistic about the future of sandbox games.

The intense criticism indicated that players were actually quite eager for this genre of games.
