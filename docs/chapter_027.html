Chapter 27: The Next Game

Yang Xin and Ruan Ningxue were full of doubts.

"Not developing a sequel to Undertale, and not making META games anymore?" Yang Xin couldn't quite understand why.

Ruan Ningxue felt the same way.

One must know that currently, game media were almost uniformly publishing various reviews about Undertale.

And in the forums frequently visited by industry designers, various professional game developers were analyzing the characteristics of Undertale's unique approach.

After all, such a fresh element truly felt like it could become a new trendsetter.

Even some game developers and designers had openly stated that they had greenlit projects incorporating META elements.

But now Chen Xu said he wouldn't do it?

It was imaginable how many people would be surprised if such news got out.

After all, the concept of META was largely ignored before Undertale.

Following the normal pattern, with the popularity of Undertale, it would be natural to strike while the iron is hot and release a sequel, just as Mirror (Game) had released DLC.

At the very least, it should be an entirely new META game!

In fact, Ruan Ningxue and Yang Xin had discussed this privately before.

They even thought that their next project might be a prequel to Undertale.

After all, in the game, the stories of Asriel and Chara were merely outlined, not fully developed.

Yet now, Chen Xu was telling them he wouldn't make a sequel to Undertale, and wouldn't even make META games anymore?

"It's not very suitable, and the scope of META elements is quite limited. Undertale certainly brought players sufficient shock, but the main reason for that was that players had no prior psychological preparation, nor any corresponding concept of META elements."

"If we continue to release META games at this point, the subsequent reactions will definitely not be better than Undertale. In fact, building on players' first impressions, they might even feel that we're getting worse and worse."

Chen Xu explained to the two of them.

Other people might not be clear about the types of META games, but Chen Xu was very clear.

Before his transmigration, META games were nothing special.

ICEY, The Stanley Parable, Pony Island, and Undertale, which Chen Xu chose to develop.

All were outstanding examples.

However, the highest sales belonged to Undertale and ICEY. The Stanley Parable, which truly brought META into players' view in his previous life, actually had lower sales than these two rising stars.

As for Pony Island, many players hadn't even heard of it.

The main reason was that META elements were not suitable as a primary core selling point.

A closer look reveals that ICEY incorporated action elements.

Although Undertale subverted RPG conventions, it was essentially a combination of RPG and bullet-hell gameplay.

Both of these games used META elements as a secondary component, while Pony Island and The Stanley Parable focused more on META elements as the primary, with other elements being secondary.

META elements could indeed be a very shocking concept for players.

However, it was not suitable as the main core; it was better suited as a lubricant, allowing it to create a chemical reaction with the game's intrinsic content.

Many large-scale games in his previous life also adopted similar designs.

For example, Psycho Mantis, the BOSS in the Metal Gear series who could read the player's memory card.

And the final ending of Nier: Automata, which utilized the player's save data to save others.

These were all uses of META elements, but mostly as a supporting feature.

And Undertale's success was certainly not solely due to META elements.

After listening to Chen Xu's explanation, Ruan Ningxue and Yang Xin were still a bit bewildered.

They understood, but not completely.

However, one point they did grasp clearly.

That is, continuing to release META games might not earn much money, and the reputation might not be good either.

Coupled with a multitude of game developers incorporating META elements, players might also experience aesthetic fatigue.

"Then for our next project, what type should we choose?" Yang Xin asked Chen Xu.

"For now, we'll make a small-cost transitional game, and the team will also need to expand," Chen Xu stated.

"As for the type... take a look at this."

Saying this, Chen Xu opened the laptop next to him and logged into a website: the Official Game Department website.

Then, under the slightly bewildered gazes of Ruan Ningxue and Yang Xin, he opened an announcement.

[Science is the source of human progress; scientific advancement and innovation are bringing about earth-shattering changes in our lives. This time, the Game Department will host a Sci-Fi Game Design Competition with the theme "Technology Changes Life."]

I. Eligible Participants: Game designers nationwide.

II. Submission Requirements: No platform restrictions, healthy and uplifting themes.

III. Event Organization: This event is hosted by the National Game Department, and selections will be made through a dual model of expert review and player voting to ensure fairness and impartiality.

IV. Selection and Recognition: Based on the total number of submitted entries, one first prize, three second prizes, six third prizes, and several honorable mentions will be awarded.

Outstanding works will be promoted and recommended on the homepage of the Official Game Platform after the event concludes.

Concurrently, winning designers will be awarded the title of "Outstanding Designer" as an encouragement.

(Registration for participation is from September 1st to September 5th, and the event concludes at the end of September.)

"Are we going to participate?" Yang Xin asked with a look of surprise.

She was actually aware of this event.

It appeared to be a call for game designers nationwide, but in reality, most participants were independent game designers or small and medium-sized game companies.

There were naturally benefits, as the host was the Game Department, which was primarily responsible for game reviews, designer professional title evaluations, game engine technology development, and the introduction of relevant game policies.

Being able to gain exposure in front of the authorities was certainly beneficial and harmless.

Furthermore, promotion and recommendations on the homepage of the Official Game Platform represented substantial benefits.

After all, the Official Game Platform was considered one of the largest channels in the country.

As for why major publishers didn't participate, it was mainly because such competitions were "chicken rib" (of little value but difficult to discard) for them.

With high costs and high production values, they certainly didn't lack such exposure.

Moreover, games with high costs and high production values would undoubtedly be primarily commercial.

Commercial-focused games naturally wouldn't lack special elements that could attract players.

Not to mention violence and fanservice, even elements like multiple female protagonists forming a harem would look very inappropriate for an official event like this.

Thus, naturally, there were no large-budget games participating.

Most participants were independent game designers and small to medium-sized game companies; of course, some studios acquired by major publishers would also participate.
