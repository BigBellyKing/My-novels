Chapter 283: Esports Planning and New Version Plans (Requesting Subscriptions, Monthly Passes)

During the period since Warcraft launched.

Aside from the story in the game, which attracted ordinary players, many players who were keen on RTS gameplay were also studying the gameplay and content of Warcraft.

Completely on par with Warcraft's single-player story, many players discovered that the RTS gameplay content of this game was far more complex than they had imagined.

In the beginning, most players basically just kept producing units, creating heroes, then fast tech-ing to all-in push.

As for anything else, they were completely baffled.

But as players gradually gained understanding, many RTS masters, leveraging the game's characteristics, discovered the impact of high-level heroes on the battle.

Moreover, heroes could also use items, and some simple tactics were invented.

For instance, in the early game, letting common soldiers accompany heroes to level up to increase hero levels faster.

At the same time, there were some targeted playstyles, such as the Orc Blademaster having Wind Walk, allowing them to harass Peasants in the enemy's base.

They would tail enemy creeping units, then use Wind Walk's bonus damage to last hit to snatch experience and items.

Furthermore, to counter early harassment from Blademasters and faster-moving Demon Hunters, some players also discovered the mysteries of Warcraft's building placement, such as blocking the path to the gold mine to prevent harassment.

In Nebula Games' office, Chen Xu was currently testing Warcraft's Map Editor.

Just then, a knock sounded on the office door. After receiving the notification, Lin Rou walked in.

‚ÄúMr. Chen, Shark TV contacted us, saying they hope to obtain authorization for a Warcraft esports event,‚Äù Lin Rou said to Chen Xu.

‚ÄúEsports event? Elaborate,‚Äù Chen Xu asked, slightly taken aback by Lin Rou's words.

The esports event aspect was actually part of Chen Xu's plan.

It was just that Chen Xu hadn't considered it for the current stage.

Mainly because the skill level of Warcraft players was still uneven.

To be honest, hosting a Warcraft tournament wouldn't have much credibility.

Moreover, for players, it was still largely a learning phase, and the spectator appeal wasn't that strong.

RTS was considered a relatively special genre among esports titles.

If one understood it, it was absolutely the most spectator-friendly.

But if one didn't understand it, it would be completely baffling.

Because even if you didn't understand esports titles like FPS and MOBA,

You could still grasp simple information like solo kills or a one-health comeback kill against one hundred health.

But what about RTS?

That involved macro aspects.

For instance, if a hero died once, some players would immediately call GG; if a few Peasants died, they'd immediately call GG.

For players who understood the game mechanics, they knew this was quite normal.

Because they were already too far behind, this amount of time was enough for the opponent to build a significant advantage and macro them to death.

But for players who didn't understand, it felt bewildering; they had no grasp of the tactics. From their perspective, one side lost a hero and three units, then suddenly surrendered. They might even suspect match-fixing.

At this stage, launching RTS competitions wasn't, in Chen Xu's opinion, a particularly good idea.

Furthermore, in the Parallel World, the esports industry wasn't as backward as one might think; on the contrary, it was quite prosperous.

Third-party event organizers, various sponsorship channels, well-regulated clubs, and corresponding guilds were all very well-established.

This also meant that there were quite a few RTS esports titles.

Although Warcraft was popular, did people want to operate esports projects for it right after launch?

With doubt, Chen Xu listened to Lin Rou's explanation and then suddenly realized.

‚ÄúShark TV wants to organize a platform event?‚Äù Chen Xu asked, looking at Lin Rou.

It was simple: the live streaming platforms saw that Warcraft's popularity was very high, and there were also many RTS masters among the streamers signed to the platform. So, they wanted to purchase a platform event authorization from Nebula Games to host a Shark TV Warcraft Monthly Tournament format, where Shark TV's contracted streamers would compete.

As for sponsorship, prizes, and operations, Shark TV would handle everything. Nebula Games would merely oversee it, glancing over things, and then collect the money.

To put it bluntly, the platform wanted to hold an event and attach an esports name to it. As for the content, it would be similar to the early internet cafe tournaments of esports.

There would be no offline audiences or offline venues.

‚ÄúContact the people at Shark TV regarding this; we can discuss it. However, we should also establish a dedicated department ourselves to first learn the relevant content and insights,‚Äù Chen Xu said to Lin Rou after some thought.

Chen Xu also valued the esports event sector highly.

It wasn't for money or tournament revenue.

What Chen Xu cared about most was the promotion of the game through such esports events.

When a game has a suitable pond (environment), esports can be a great way to consolidate player stickiness and IP.

For example, in his previous life, games like League of Legends and DOTA ‚Äî even if their player numbers decreased, the overall IP value of the games did not decline at all.

Because many players would still watch matches even if they didn't play the game, and for Warcraft, cultivating such IP value was clearly very necessary.

Of course, it was also important to strike a balance: third-party events and official events should proceed simultaneously.

Like Blizzard in his previous life, if you say they had the ability, they wanted to monopolize profits, which led to StarCraft 2 falling out with third-party organizers during its golden age, squandering a good hand.

But if you say they lacked ability, for games like Overwatch, whose popularity continued to decline, the clubs and events were still managed well, even to the point where everyone had a livelihood.

This collaboration with Shark TV, besides further promoting the game, also aimed to cultivate Nebula Games' internal understanding of the esports industry.

After all, Nebula Games would inevitably develop this type of game in the future.

And esports-type games, if operated well, were essentially evergreens.

As for how Shark TV would actually organize the first Warcraft 'internet cafe tournament', that could be discussed later.

After speaking with Lin Rou, Chen Xu also sent a message in the work group chat.

Asking HR to see if there were any employees willing to transfer positions, and to recruit new staff.
