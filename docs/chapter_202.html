Chapter 302: Some Content Changes (7th update, requesting subscriptions and monthly passes)

In terms of game mechanics, the modes in Rainbow Six Siege are actually quite straightforward.

They are mainly divided into attackers and defenders.

Among these, there are three different modes:

Hostage Mode, Bomb Mode, and Secure Area Mode.

Hostage Mode and Bomb Mode are generally present in previous FPS games, with the main differences lying in some mechanical adjustments.

As for Secure Area Mode, it is essentially a point-capture mode where victory is declared when either team's players occupy a specific area.

Besides these, there's also a rather special mode called Terrorist Hunt Mode.

This mode is mainly for new players to train, allowing them to single-handedly or team up against AI terrorist personnel of varying difficulty levels.

Of course, this mode was later renamed Training Grounds, and the suicide bombers within it also disappeared.

Regarding this mode, Chen Xu still decided to retain the earlier Terrorist Hunt Mode. Furthermore, by utilizing AI technology, players could experience a more realistic counter-terrorism feel when facing the White Masks in PVE.

The rest concerns the recreation of maps. For Rainbow Six Siege, maps are arguably the most crucial core element.

In his previous life, many players claimed "500 hours to get started," which made many rookies tremble, but in reality, it was a rather exaggerated statement.

Because unlike other FPS games, Rainbow Six Siege doesn't demand as high a level of marksmanship as others.

More importantly, it's about familiarity with the operators' skill mechanisms, gadget usage, and the maps themselves.

If one isn't familiar with the maps, a common situation arises:

Who am I?

Where am I?

Who's shooting me?

Who killed me again?

How did someone get behind me?

Rainbow Six Siege is practically turned into a horror game by sheer unfamiliarity with the maps.

This is precisely what results from a lack of map familiarity.

Because in Rainbow Six Siege, walls, wooden panels, and other elements are destructible. Once destroyed, a new attack route emerges.

Attackers can use their gadgets to forcibly destroy obstacles, launching unexpected attacks to catch opponents off guard.

Similarly, defenders can exploit this feature to create passages first, expanding their line of sight, or they can fortify themselves in a small room. Of course, this is strongly discouraged unless it's for specific maps.

Additionally, attackers possess two drones that can enter the building through gaps beneath wooden boards and ventilation ducts to observe the situation and gather intelligence.

Defenders, on the other hand, gain access to the building's camera feeds, allowing them to monitor information from various areas.

It can be said that from the very beginning, both sides of the game will enter a phase of information gathering and psychological warfare.

Secondly, in terms of action, because it's a VR game, Chen Xu also considered making it more realistic.

Of course, there will be two versions: one for ordinary players and a military version.

However, the two versions will have some differences.

The military version will include more detailed operational content.

For instance, enabling hostages in Hostage Mode to move.

After all, in a real counter-terrorism operation, hostages wouldn't just be crouched on the ground, immobile with their hands over their heads.

Secondly, there's the more realistic operation of being able to step over tripwires, including more destructible environments and interactive items.

Also, character jumping in the game.

In his previous life, some players also raised questions about this, and the official answer was that it was for realism; after all, how could counter-terrorism personnel jump while carrying tens of pounds of equipment?

This answer seemed relatively reasonable, but in reality, it was simply to reduce workload and bugs.

Chen Xu, however, plans to incorporate jumping into the military version.

Because compared to the civilian version, the military version can focus more on realism rather than balance.

After all, isn't the direction given by the Game Department for training purposes?

And honestly, jumping doesn't have much significance in Rainbow Six Siege.

The imagined scenario of a jumping Fuze deploying a 'thump-thump-thump' on the ceiling is entirely non-existent.

After all, carrying such heavy equipment, even if one could jump, it wouldn't be very high, at most just a small hop.

Furthermore, the version provided to players will also have many changes in minor details.

For example, rope climbing. Camera angle switching in VR is also a critical core point; otherwise, Rainbow Six might very well turn into Vertigo Six.

..................

Chen Xu was gradually perfecting the design concept document for Rainbow Six Siege.

He kept a relatively low profile, but NetDragon, having already confirmed its cooperation with Nebula Games, became very high-profile after their discussions.

They specifically held a news conference to announce the latest news about co-developing a VR blockbuster with Nebula Games.

[The new game will be co-developed by Nebula Games and NetDragon! The codename is temporarily set as 'R6']

[This will be a game utilizing the latest VR technology, bringing an unprecedented new experience to all players]

[Nebula Games Platform and NetDragon Platform will engage in deep strategic cooperation]

Instantly, news flew everywhere.

There was official news revealed by NetDragon, various unofficial news, and wild guesses from players.

As for Chen Xu, he kept a much lower profile, only posting on the official Weibo account to confirm that the game codenamed 'R6' was indeed real.

In an instant, the gaming industry experienced a major upheaval, with countless industry designers and developers looking bewildered.

NetDragon cooperated with Nebula Games!?

Didn't NetDragon seem to be making overtures to the Channel Alliance before?

How did they suddenly turn around and get together with Nebula Games?

The Channel Alliance, in particular, was dumbfounded.

NetDragon's attitude had initially been vague, unlike TengHua, but they had assumed NetDragon was "one of their own," and that with a little effort, they could win them over.

But now, they'd turned around and run off to the competitor?

And it seemed like NetDragon had even initiated the move?

But compared to the industry's deep shock over NetDragon and Nebula Games entering into a strategic partnership...

Ordinary players clearly didn't care about any of that; what truly concerned them was that NetDragon and Nebula Games were actually jointly developing a VR blockbuster!?

As soon as this news was announced, players of both NetDragon and Nebula Games instantly erupted in excitement.

After all, while NetDragon hasn't been doing so well in recent years, it's still a giant in the gaming industry.

And Nebula Games, needless to say, has released nothing but quality titles so far.

The recently popular Warcraft hadn't even lost its momentum yet!

The remaining question was, what type and theme would this game, codenamed R6, actually be?
