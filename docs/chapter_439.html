Song Ren wasn't particularly surprised by the puzzles. Essentially, most games, be they action, FPS, or any other genre, use puzzles as a seasoning.

Why do game developers like to include them? The answer is simple: they can extend playtime. However, puzzles are a double-edged sword. If you do them poorly, players won't appreciate it and will leave negative reviews. If you do them well, players will give high ratings, but if they're too difficult, they might turn players away. After all, not all players are Sherlock Holmes.

It's like the puzzles in The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild. If you say they aren't hard, some shrine levels will certainly make players scratch their heads. But if you say they are hard, it's really just a matter of perspective. Moreover, The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild has a very clever point compared to typical puzzle games: most of the puzzle elements are linked to the various abilities players use. This means solving puzzles while playing, rather than pure puzzle-solving, and the game's guidance is also quite excellent.

Uncharted, on the other hand, leans towards more traditional puzzles. This involves combining mechanisms and clues for players to decipher. Thankfully, they aren't too difficult, and the game provides corresponding hints.
