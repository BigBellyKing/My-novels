Inside the Hunter's Lodge, Zhang Yi was utterly spoilt for choice, gazing at the array of weapons before him.

Just like the previously revealed information, there are no distinct classes in Monster Hunter: World. Players can choose any weapon as their own and switch between them at any time.

However, while switching is free, much like Dark Souls, later-game weapons require materials for crafting and enhancement.

Furthermore, it's currently unknown if the game has a system for disassembling items to recover materials. If not, it's clear that for a period, one would have to stick with a single chosen weapon.

After all, in the early stages, there wouldn't be enough materials to craft and upgrade every weapon.

And if you don't craft or upgrade, you'd be stuck with low-level weapons, and your hunting damage would be negligible.

Therefore, it's crucial to carefully select a weapon you're comfortable with.

On the left side, there was something resembling a move list, and after attacking, follow-up combo guides would appear in the upper right corner.

Zhang Yi quickly realized that, compared to Dark Souls, the combat mode and system in Monster Hunter: World were completely different.

The entire action system was relatively much more complex, and the game also featured two distinct states: combat and non-combat.

In combat state, players hold their weapons and cannot sprint, only dodge by rolling.

When entering the non-combat state, players put away their weapons, and then they can sprint.

Next were the attack combinations, which felt somewhat like inputting commands in a fighting game.

It required players to string together various skills through attacks to deal damage to monsters.

And to fulfill specific prerequisites before certain moves could be activated.

Taking the Long Sword in his hand as an example, the basic ordinary attack methods were just a few.

There were regular Slashes, Thrusts, and after accumulating the Spirit Gauge through these ordinary attacks, one could use the more flashy Spirit Blade.

For higher-level moves, such as Helm Breaker, one needed to maintain the Spirit Gauge State during a Spirit Thrust. To fill the Spirit Gauge, players could choose to use the Foresight Slash (also known as a Parry) at the very moment of receiving an attack to parry the monster's attack.

If successful, it would rapidly fill the Spirit Gauge, or one could diligently use Slashes and Thrusts to accumulate the Spirit Gauge, then use Spirit Blade III combined with the final Spirit Roundslash to fill the gauge.

After trying the Long Sword, Zhang Yi then tried other weapons.

Among them, the Charge Blade was the weapon he found the most complex.

For a regular Amped Element Discharge, players first needed to accumulate phials in Sword Mode. Once the phials were full, they could unleash the 'Amped Element Discharge' which carried lightning.

However, to unleash the even more explosive Super Amped Element Discharge, players needed to interrupt the 'Amped Element Discharge' by charging power into the shield, and only then could they unleash the world-destroying 'Super Amped Element Discharge'.

Although the damage was high and it looked cool, Zhang Yi felt that he would likely forget these steps when actually fighting.

Every weapon has very unique characteristics.

After careful consideration, Zhang Yi ultimately chose the Great Sword.

As for the reason?

Initially, he had indeed intended to pick the Long Sword, as it was simply too cool.

But after some thought, he gave it up.

As an experienced gamer, Zhang Yi understood game design quite well.

That is, when a weapon is incredibly cool, it's almost certainly not the strongest.

Why?

The Long Sword was already so stylish; its appearance, design, and moves alone could attract a huge number of players.

So, if the Long Sword was also very powerful in actual combat, what would be the point of all the other weapons?

Nebula Games had designed so many weapons; wouldn't they all become useless?

Therefore, the Long Sword might not be the weakest weapon, but it definitely wouldn't be considered strong.

But the Great Sword was different!

Seemingly unadorned, yet boundless in its majesty.

Moreover, during testing, the description mentioned 'Super Armor'.

As is well known, in any action game, as long as there's Super Armor, it won't be particularly weak.

When he tested its damage, the Great Sword was also top-tier, with a single True Charged Slash dealing over a hundred damage.

Only the Long Sword's Helm Breaker and the Charge Blade's Super Amped Element Discharge could rival such brutal performance.

But he felt that the Great Sword's charging was a bit simpler.

As for skills like Blocking and Tackle, he ignored them.
